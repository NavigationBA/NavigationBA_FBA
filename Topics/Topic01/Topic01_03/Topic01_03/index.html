    Topic 1.3 - 商业分析的伦理问题¶

    1. 商业分析中的伦理¶

    Business analytics is not just a technical activity — it is also a human one.

    • Every decision about what data to collect, how to analyze it, and how to act on it involves ethical considerations.
    • A technically sound system can still produce socially harmful results. A model optimizing for efficiency may overlook fairness. A dashboard enabling faster decisions might expose sensitive information.
    • Ethics means asking not just "can we build it?" but "should we? Who benefits? What risks are introduced?"

    Core questions:

    • Are we using data in ways that respect people's rights?
    • Could our models unintentionally reinforce unfairness?
    • Who is accountable when analytics go wrong?
    • Who benefits — or loses — when analytics drive decisions?

    We evaluate data-driven solutions through four ethical lenses:

    1. Privacy — Collecting and using data in ways that respect individual control and confidentiality.
    2. Bias — Ensuring models perform fairly across groups without systematically disadvantaging some.
    3. Accountability — Establishing clear ownership, explainable systems, and recourse paths when problems occur.
    4. Power — Understanding who gains, who bears risks, and whose voice matters in analytics-informed decisions.

    案例：LearnLoop 的早期预警系统

    LearnLoop is a Learning Management System developing an Early Warning System (EWS) that identifies students at risk of falling behind, enabling timely intervention to improve retention and pass rates.

    • The initial prototype uses LMS activity data — logins, assignment submissions, page engagement time — to estimate disengagement.
    • The team explores enhancing predictive power through additional datasets: academic performance history (GPA, past failures), demographic information (age, gender, enrollment mode, international status), and course-level risk factors.

    Key challenges:

    • Who decides which variables are appropriate?
    • Should students be notified about or consulted regarding secondary data uses?
    • How can the system avoid reinforcing disadvantage or making unfair assumptions?

    2. 商业分析伦理问题的四个视角¶

    (1) 视角一：隐私 - Privacy¶

    (a) 商业分析中的隐私¶

    Privacy is not just about secrecy — it's about control. It concerns respecting people's right to understand and control how information about them is used — not just the information they gave you, but also what you infer about them through analysis.

    Key questions:

    • Are students aware of what data is being collected?
    • Is the data necessary, or just "nice to have"?
    • Could this data be used to infer sensitive traits (e.g. mental health)?

    Privacy risks often arise when:

    • Data collected for one purpose is reused for another
    • Sensitive inferences are made without transparency or consent
    • Systems gather more than they need

    Good privacy practice involves:

    • Collecting only what's necessary for a clear purpose
    • Limiting access to sensitive information
    • Designing transparent systems about how personal data is used
    • Building trust through respectful data handling

    (b) 隐私的法律与道德层面¶

    While "notice and consent" might satisfy legal requirements, it rarely reflects meaningful consent. Most users don't read privacy policies. Even when they do, language is often vague, legalistic, or overly broad.

    • Secondary data uses may be technically permissible under broad consent agreements while still breaching user expectations and eroding trust.
    • Ethical question: Were users given enough context to make informed choices? Would they be surprised or upset learning how their data is actually used?

    (c) 隐私与个性化¶

    Personalization is often justified as helping users more effectively. While sometimes true, personalization always requires profiling, and detailed profiling becomes increasingly intrusive.

    • Being labeled "at risk" can feel stigmatizing, particularly if students never consented to that classification, never saw supporting data, and had no opportunity to contest it.
    • Key principle: Helpful analytics must also be respectful analytics — especially when they deal with sensitive aspects of people's lives.

    Case Study: Spotify's Mood Detection Patent (2021)

    Spotify received a patent enabling inference of listeners' emotional state, gender, and accent from voice and background noise.

    Critics asked:

    • Was it clear to users that emotions were being inferred?
    • Could this data be repurposed for targeted advertising?

    This demonstrates how quickly personalisation can blur into surveillance.

    (d) 隐私与匿名化¶

    Anonymization provides incomplete privacy protection. De-identified data can often be re-identified, particularly with detailed behavioral patterns or when combined with other datasets.

    Even without student names, the model might use:

    • Unique login times (single student studying only at 2am)
    • Unusual course combinations
    • Repeated help-seeking patterns

    These act as digital fingerprints. Risk is especially high in small cohorts or when involving sensitive groups.

    Key principle: Privacy risk lives in context, not just columns. Whether data looks anonymous in spreadsheets matters less than whether real-world re-identification is reasonably possible.

    Anonymization Method Function Effectiveness
    Removing names/IDs Deletes obvious identifiers Not sufficient alone
    Masking rare traits Removes/groups rare values Helpful but imperfect
    K-anonymity Makes each record resemble several others Better for structured data
    L-diversity / T-closeness Protects against sensitive info guessing Adds robustness
    Differential Privacy Adds random noise to aggregate data Most rigorous option
    Synthetic data Creates fake data mimicking original patterns Safe when well-designed

    (e) 暗模式：当设计破坏用户选择¶

    Dark patterns nudge users toward particular actions without full understanding or consent. Example: An automated message states "We noticed signs you might be falling behind. Click here to accept academic support." This seems helpful but may:

    • Lack clear explanation of "falling behind"
    • Induce anxiety or shame even when students perform adequately
    • Offer only "Accept Support" without decline, clarification, or data visibility options

    Principle: Ethical analytics extends beyond predictions to interface design — ensuring messages are clear and balanced, choices are genuine, and users don't feel manipulated.

    澳大利亚隐私法规¶

    The Privacy Act 1988 and Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) apply to most organizations handling personal information. Most relevant principles for business analytics:

    • Transparency and anonymity: Clear privacy policies must exist; anonymous use options should be available.
    • Purpose limitation: Data collected only when necessary for clear business functions.
    • Use and disclosure: Data used/shared only for original collection purposes unless exceptions apply.
    • Security and data retention: Reasonable protective steps required; data destroyed or de-identified when no longer needed.
    • Access and correction: Individuals can access and correct their personal information.
    • Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) Scheme: When serious breaches occur, organizations must notify affected individuals and report to the OAIC.

    (2) 视角二：偏差与公平性 - Bias and Fairness¶

    什么是分析中的偏差？¶

    Even if data is collected ethically, it can still lead to unfair outcomes. Bias occurs when analysis produces systematic errors that disproportionately affect certain groups of people.

    A model with 85% overall accuracy might achieve 95% for domestic students but only 65% for international students — a serious fairness problem hidden by aggregate metrics.

    偏差的来源¶

    Bias can creep in at multiple stages of the pipeline:

    • Data (who's missing?): Underrepresentation causes models to perform worse on missing groups. If the dataset contains mostly full-time business students, the model won't learn patterns for part-time or remote students.
    • Variables (what are you really measuring?): Proxy variables can encode protected attributes even when excluded. ZIP code may encode socioeconomic status; login timing may disadvantage students working night shifts.
    • Labels (are past outcomes fair?): Using historical drop-out data assumes past outcomes were fair. If certain students received less support and dropped out more, the model learns to treat them as inherently "risky" — reproducing past injustice with mathematical legitimacy.
    • Drift (fairness over time): Initially fair models can become biased as real-world patterns change across semesters or new student cohorts.

    Case Study: Apple's Credit Card and Gender Bias (2019) Users reported Apple's credit card offered men significantly higher limits than women, even with identical qualifications. The algorithm included no gender variable but used proxy features (occupation, income patterns) that acted as gender proxies. Lesson: Excluding protected attributes doesn't eliminate bias if proxies still encode structural inequalities.

    Case Study: Amazon's Hiring Algorithm (2018) Amazon shut down an internal hiring algorithm that downgraded applicants from all-women's colleges. Trained on 10-year resume data, the system replicated historical bias. The system inferred gender from past hiring patterns without explicitly including it.

    公平性指标¶

    No single fairness definition exists. Different contexts require different metrics:

    Metric Question Description
    Group Parity (Demographic Parity) Are people in different groups flagged at the same rate? Checks whether the proportion flagged is similar across groups. Limitation: doesn't reveal prediction correctness.
    Error Parity (Equalised Odds) Are error rates similar across groups? Checks false positive and false negative rates across groups. Widely used because it reflects actual outcomes.
    Calibration Do predicted risk scores mean the same thing for different groups? Checks whether a risk score of 0.8 means the same risk level regardless of group membership.

    Trade-offs: Fairness metrics sometimes conflict. Improving one (group parity) might worsen another (calibration). Fairness choices involve judgment grounded in values, business context, and affected populations.

    Case Study: COMPAS Recidivism Prediction U.S. courts used COMPAS to predict reoffense likelihood. Black defendants were more likely to be falsely flagged as high-risk; white defendants were more likely to be falsely flagged as low-risk. The company argued the model was calibrated, but critics argued it violated equalised odds. Key lesson: Fairness metrics conflict, and choosing among them involves ethical judgment.

    偏差审计工作流¶

    A 6-step framework for fairness auditing:

    1. Define the decision — What outcome does the analysis support? Who is affected?
    2. Identify relevant groups — Which groups might be treated differently? (full-time vs. part-time, domestic vs. international, disability status)
    3. Choose a fairness metric — What does fairness mean here: equal outcomes, equal errors, or consistent scores?
    4. Slice model performance — Break down key metrics (false positives, accuracy, risk scores) by group.
    5. Flag gaps and trade-offs — Where are biggest disparities? Are they explainable or fixable?
    6. Propose mitigation and monitoring — What could reduce harm? How will fairness be monitored over time?

    (3) 视角三：问责 - Accountability¶

    什么是分析中的问责？¶

    A model can be accurate and fair — but still feel unaccountable. Accountability means knowing who's responsible for outcomes and being able to explain what happened when problems occur.

    Key questions:

    • Who is responsible for how a system was designed?
    • Who is accountable for what the system decides?
    • Can users understand and contest those decisions?
    • How will system drift, failure, or misuse be detected?

    Without clear ownership, explanations, and oversight, even a well-meaning system can harm trust and lead to unfair outcomes.

    "Accountability isn't just about blame — it's about building trustworthy systems that are answerable to the people they impact."

    角色与责任：RACI 模型¶

    To clarify accountability, organizations use the RACI Model:

    Role Description
    Responsible Who builds and runs the system?
    Accountable Who ultimately owns outcomes and signs off on decisions?
    Consulted Who needs involvement in decisions? (legal, ethics, data protection)
    Informed Who needs updates? (customer support, communications)

    Clear role assignment prevents "no one told me" scenarios and creates decision-approval records.

    可解释性 vs 透明度¶

    Related but distinct concepts:

    • Explainability = Making system logic understandable. Can someone grasp why a particular outcome occurred?
    • Transparency = Access and openness. Can people see what data was used? How does the system work?

    A transparent system may still be hard to explain. An explainable system might not be transparent about data selection or weighting. Both matter.

    Case Study: Target's Pregnancy Prediction Model (2010s) Target developed a model identifying pregnant customers through buying behavior changes. The model mailed maternity coupons to customers — sometimes before family knowledge. One reported case involved a teenager. The model wasn't explainable to affected people, use of the prediction wasn't transparent, and no clear consent or control existed. Lesson: Being "right" technically isn't sufficient.

    (4) 视角四：权力 - Power¶

    什么是商业分析中的权力？¶

    Even when systems are private, fair, and accountable, they can reinforce unequal outcomes by shifting power toward those measuring and away from those measured.

    • Business analytics don't merely describe — they shape decisions, determining who gains, who loses, and whose voice matters.
    • Systems that collect and act on data can reinforce power imbalances between institutions and individuals.

    Power distribution in analytics is uneven:

    • Sometimes analytics empowers people through increased control, opportunity discovery, or fairer decisions.
    • Sometimes they concentrate power by reinforcing hierarchies, disempowering monitored individuals, or enabling decision-making without visibility or recourse.

    Ethical power analysis considers:

    • Whose outcomes are optimized?
    • Who bears downside risk if models fail?
    • What mechanisms give affected people voice?

    Case Study: Wi-Fi Surveillance at an Australian University (2019) Students discovered campus Wi-Fi tracked movements through linked MAC addresses. Originally used for network planning, it was repurposed during a protest to estimate attendance and identify who entered certain buildings. Students hadn't been informed about this possible use, with no opt-out options. This highlights how analytics systems often concentrate power in ways invisible until something goes wrong.

    操控与信任¶

    Power risks include:

    • Manipulation: nudging or coercing users toward actions they didn't intend.
    • Surveillance: using data to monitor or discipline users in ways they can't challenge.
    • Voice and agency: systems that make decisions without input from the people affected.

    Design principles supporting trust:

    • Inform rather than pressure
    • Respect user autonomy rather than override it
    • Support agency, especially for vulnerable or lower-power users

    "Trust is a strategic asset. Once lost, it's hard to recover — and without it, even the smartest model will fail to deliver impact."

    Case Study: TikTok's "For You" Algorithm TikTok's personalized feed learns from every scroll, like, pause, and replay. Some users become trapped in feedback loops reinforcing harmful content. Limited visibility into why content appears, with minimal user control. The ethical question: Does the algorithm optimize for user well-being or just engagement? Power asymmetry: TikTok holds data, algorithms, and commercial incentives, while users are persistently nudged with minimal transparency.

    3. 商业分析伦理问题小结¶

    Lens Key Question Focus
    Privacy Are we collecting and using data respectfully? Control, consent, proportionality, anonymization
    Bias Is the system fair for all groups? Data representation, proxy variables, fairness metrics
    Accountability Who is responsible, and is the system explainable? RACI roles, explainability, transparency, contestability
    Power How does this system shape choices or influence? Manipulation, surveillance, agency, trust
